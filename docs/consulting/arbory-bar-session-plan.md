# Arbory Bar — Access Consulting Session Plan

**Client:** Arbory Bar, Melbourne (Flinders Walk, riverside)
**Consultant:** Flare Access
**Focus:** Marketing a newly accessible experience
**Secondary:** Digital accessibility
**Suggested duration:** 2 hours
**Format:** Discussion, worksheet, action list

---

## 1. Opening and Context Setting (10 min)

- Confirm what has changed physically and operationally at Arbory (ramp, bathroom, staff training, menu formats, seating, service animal policy, etc.)
- Clarify "digi" scope: website accessibility, social media, booking flow, Google Business Profile, or digital content accessibility?
- Confirm target audiences: locals, tourists, corporate bookings, events, community groups?
- Define success: bookings, walk-ins, reviews, media coverage, repeat visits, community reputation?

---

## 2. Positioning the Accessible Experience (15 min)

**Core principle:** Market the experience, not the compliance. Patrons with disability want a great night out, not a checklist.

**Positioning options to workshop:**

- **"Yarra-side without compromise"** -- lean into Arbory's iconic riverfront identity and show that accessibility extends the existing promise
- **"Melbourne's most welcoming riverside bar"** -- warmth-led positioning with broader appeal
- **Specific and specific** -- name exactly what is accessible (step-free entry from Flinders Walk, accessible bathroom on main level, large-print menus, quiet seating zone)

**The rule:** Specificity builds trust. "Accessible venue" is weak. "Step-free entry via Flinders Walk, accessible bathroom on main level, staff trained in Auslan basics and low-vision service" is strong.

---

## 3. The Access Information Asset Stack (20 min)

Every channel should point back to a single source of truth. Build these in priority order:

### 3.1 Access Page on the Website
A dedicated URL (e.g. `/access` or `/accessibility`) covering:
- Entry details and path of travel
- Bathroom location and features
- Seating options (height, spacing, quiet zones)
- Menu formats available (large print, digital, picture menu)
- Sensory environment (typical noise levels by time of day, music volume, lighting)
- Service animal welcome policy
- Staff training summary
- Contact for specific access needs (phone and email, not just a form)

### 3.2 Access Photos
- Entry point and path of travel
- Bathroom interior
- Table heights and spacing
- Any ramps, lifts or level changes
- Signage and wayfinding

Photos beat words. Show the real space, not a staged version.

### 3.3 Short Video Walkthrough (60 to 90 seconds)
- First-person perspective from arrival to seated
- Hosted on YouTube with captions, embedded on the access page
- Huge trust signal for people planning a visit

### 3.4 Google Business Profile
- Tick every accessibility attribute available
- Add access photos to the listing
- Mention accessibility in the business description
- Seed the Q&A with real questions ("Is there step-free entry?", "Can I bring my guide dog?")

### 3.5 Booking Flow
- Add an open text field: "Anything we should know to make your visit great?"
- Open text, not a dropdown of disabilities. Let people tell you what they need in their own words.

## 5. Digital Accessibility (15 min)

Marketing accessibility while having an inaccessible website is the number one credibility killer. This section addresses the "digi" topic.

### Website Checklist
- Alt text on all images (especially the new access photos)
- Captions on every video (auto-generated then human-corrected)
- Colour contrast meets WCAG AA: 4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text
- Keyboard navigation works end-to-end, including the booking flow
- Headings structured properly (H1, H2, H3 in order)
- Link text describes the destination ("View our menu" not "click here")
- Forms have visible labels and clear error messages
- PDFs (menus, event flyers) are tagged and screen-reader accessible, or offer an HTML alternative

### Social Media Checklist
- Alt text on all Instagram and Facebook images
- Captions on all video content (burned in, not auto-only)
- CamelCase hashtags (#AccessibleMelbourne not #accessiblemelbourne) so screen readers parse them correctly
- Avoid conveying information only through colour or images
- Stories and reels: use text overlays with sufficient contrast

### Free Audit Tools
- WAVE browser extension (wave.webaim.org)
- axe DevTools browser extension
- Accessibility Insights for Web (Microsoft)
- Lighthouse in Chrome DevTools (Accessibility audit)

### Specialist Support
For a professional digital accessibility audit or remediation, contact Flare Access (flareaccess.com.au) or Vision Australia's digital access team.

## 6. Language and Tone Guide (5 min)

**Use:**
- "People with disability" (Australian preferred terminology)
- "Accessible"
- "Welcoming"
- Specific feature descriptions
- "Assistance animal" (legal term in Australia)

**Avoid:**
- "Suffers from", "wheelchair-bound", "confined to a wheelchair"
- "Special needs", "differently-abled", "handicapped"
- Inspiration language ("brave", "inspiring" applied to everyday activities)
- "Despite their disability"

**Important:** Do not feature only one type of disability in marketing. Disability is broad: sensory, cognitive, physical, psychosocial, chronic illness, ambulant mobility. Representation should reflect that breadth.

## 7. Connecting with Disability Service Providers and Community Groups (15 min)

Disability communities are tight-knit and trust-driven. A recommendation from a known organisation carries more weight than any advertisement. Build relationships, not just marketing reach.

### Deaf and Hard of Hearing

| Organisation | How to Engage |
|---|---|
| **Deaf Connect** (deafconnect.org.au) | Largest Deaf services provider nationally. Promote through their community networks. |
| **Expression Australia** (expression.com.au) | Melbourne-based (South Melbourne), Deaf-led, established 1884. Can advise on Deaf-friendly venue practices and connect to community. |
| **Deaf Victoria** (deafvictoria.org.au) | State advocacy body. Promote accessible events through their channels. |
| **Auslan Stage Left** (auslanstageleft.com.au) | Auslan interpreters for live events, music, comedy. If Arbory hosts live acts, this is the partner to make them Deaf-accessible. 3,000+ events nationally. |

**Quick win:** Book Auslan Stage Left for one live event. Promote it through Deaf Connect and Expression Australia. One event builds a lasting reputation in the Deaf community.

### Autism and Neurodivergent

| Organisation | How to Engage |
|---|---|
| **Amaze** (amaze.org.au) | Victoria's autism peak body, based in Richmond. Runs Autism Connect helpline. Can advise on sensory-friendly practices and promote through their networks. |
| **I CAN Network** (icannetwork.online) | Autistic-led mentoring organisation, Melbourne-based. Can deliver staff awareness training. |
| **Yellow Ladybugs** (yellowladybugs.com.au) | Supports autistic girls, women and gender-diverse people. Strong community voice for authentic promotion and co-design. |
| **Reframing Autism** (reframingautism.org.au) | Autistic-led acceptance and inclusion organisation. Can amplify through their engaged audience. |
| **Aspect** (aspect.org.au) | Australia's largest autism-specific service provider. Melbourne office in Northcote. Runs an Autism Friendly Accreditation program for venues. |

**Quick win:** Contact Amaze for a sensory walkthrough of the venue. Their feedback becomes both a practical checklist and a community endorsement.

### Vision Impairment

| Organisation | How to Engage |
|---|---|
| **Vision Australia** (visionaustralia.org) | Can advise on large-print and braille menus, lighting, wayfinding, digital accessibility. Multiple Melbourne locations. |
| **Guide Dogs Victoria** (vic.guidedogs.com.au) | Confirm and promote assistance animal welcome policy. They can share with their handler networks. |
| **Blind Citizens Australia** (bca.org.au) | Consumer advocacy organisation. Promote through their community channels and New Horizons radio. |

### Physical and Mobility

| Organisation | How to Engage |
|---|---|
| **Scope** (scopeaust.org.au) | Victoria's largest disability service provider, 425+ locations. Community participation programs actively looking for inclusive venues. |
| **Yooralla** (yooralla.com.au) | Over 100 years in Melbourne. Community hubs and programs. Could host a group outing to Arbory. |
| **Spinal Life Australia** (spinal.com.au) | Runs the Accessible Australia App where patrons review venues. Get Arbory listed and reviewed. |

### Intellectual Disability

| Organisation | How to Engage |
|---|---|
| **VALID** (valid.org.au) | Runs 4 self-advocacy networks across Melbourne. Can advise on Easy Read menus and clear signage. Direct channel to community members. |
| **Inclusion Melbourne** (inclusionmelbourne.org.au) | Social inclusion programs, actively seeking welcoming community venues for participants. |

### Mental Health and Psychosocial

| Organisation | How to Engage |
|---|---|
| **SANE Australia** (sane.org) | Melbourne-based. Education and advocacy for complex mental health. Their StigmaWatch work is relevant for how a venue communicates about mental health. |
| **Mind Australia** (mindaustralia.org.au) | One of Australia's largest psychosocial support providers. Community programs looking for inclusive social venues. |

### Cross-Disability and Practical Partners

| Organisation | How to Engage |
|---|---|
| **Travellers Aid** (travellersaid.org.au) | Located at Flinders Street Station, directly adjacent to Arbory. Motorised wheelchair hire, free companion escort service (72 hours notice). The most practical partner for getting patrons to the venue. |
| **NDIS Local Area Coordinators** | Brotherhood of St Laurence and Latrobe Community Health Service cover inner Melbourne. Inform them Arbory is accessible so they can refer NDIS participants seeking social activities. |
| **DARU** (daru.org.au) | Disability Advocacy Resource Unit. Best starting point for understanding the Victorian advocacy landscape and making the right connections. |

### Engagement Approach

Do not treat these organisations as marketing channels. The approach should be:

1. **Invite, do not pitch.** Offer a complimentary experience for staff or community members so they can assess the venue themselves.
2. **Ask for feedback.** Genuine openness to criticism builds trust faster than a polished pitch.
3. **Co-design where possible.** If creating a sensory-friendly session, involve autistic people in designing it. If creating an Easy Read menu, involve people with intellectual disability in testing it.
4. **Sustain the relationship.** One event or one invitation is a start. Ongoing partnership (quarterly events, standing invitations, feedback loops) is what builds lasting reputation.

## 8. Promoting the Sensory-Inclusive Experience (15 min)

### Where to Get Listed and Certified

| Platform | What It Is | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| **Autism SA Autism Friendly Charter** (autismsa.org.au/autism-friendly-charter) | Free online training. Join the charter and get listed in a searchable national directory and the Autism Friendly app. | Free |
| **Aspect Autism Friendly Accreditation** (aspect.org.au) | Formal accreditation with staff training, quality assurance, and visible certification. Used by venues, transport, retail. | Paid |
| **KultureCity** (kulturecity.org) | Global sensory inclusive certification. 6,000+ venues in 40 countries. Provides sensory bags, staff training, and app listing. | Paid |
| **Be Like Buddy** (belikebuddy.com) | Australian directory of sensory-friendly businesses and events. | Free listing |
| **Accessibull Eats** (accessibulleats.com.au) | Hospitality-specific accessibility guidelines across 5 categories (visual, auditory, mobility, cognitive, sensory). Has a Quick Fix Checklist. | Free resources |
| **Accessible Australia App** (Spinal Life Australia) | Crowd-sourced venue reviews for accessibility. Covers bars, restaurants, hotels. | Free listing |

### Where to Promote

| Channel | Action |
|---|---|
| **What's On Melbourne** (whatson.melbourne.vic.gov.au) | City of Melbourne's official site. Has accessible venue guides and a Disability Pride Guide. Submit for inclusion. |
| **Visit Victoria - Accessible Victoria** (visitvictoria.com) | State tourism body's accessible tourism section. Pursue listing. |
| **GetAboutAble** (getaboutable.com) | Australian accessible venue directory with national and international listings. Submit listing. |
| **Access Australia Online** (accessaustraliaonline.com.au) | National directory filtered by location and type. |
| **That Disability Adventure Company** (thatdisabilityadventurecompany.com.au) | Runs accessible activities and blogs about accessible Melbourne. Partner for events or get featured. |

### What Makes a Credible Sensory-Inclusive Experience

**Sensory map:** Create a simple visual map of the venue showing noise zones, quiet zones, lighting levels, speaker locations, kitchen noise areas, and crowd pinch points. Display on the access page and print for the venue.

**Sensory kit:** Make available at the bar on request:
- Noise-reducing earbuds or ear defenders
- Fidget tools
- Sunglasses
- Visual menu card (pictures and simple text)
- Communication card ("I need a moment", "Quieter please", "I'm okay")

**Quiet hours or low-sensory sessions:**
- Reduced music volume
- Dimmed or softened lighting
- Staff briefed to allow extra time, not rush, check in without hovering
- Even one session per week or fortnight builds a strong reputation
- Promote through Amaze, Yellow Ladybugs, Reframing Autism, Aspect

**Social story:** A step-by-step visual guide on the website showing what to expect from arrival to leaving. Include photos of the entry, where to go, what the space looks like, how to order, where the bathroom is, and how to leave. Widely used for autism, also helpful for anxiety, intellectual disability, and anyone visiting a new place.

**Staff training:** Even a 30-minute awareness session changes everything. Staff who recognise sensory overload and respond calmly (offering a quiet spot, reducing stimulation, giving space) are the product. Contact Amaze, I CAN Network, or Aspect for training options.

### Recommended Priority Order

1. Join the Autism SA Autism Friendly Charter (free, immediate, gets national directory listing)
2. Contact Amaze for a venue walkthrough and staff awareness session
3. Create a sensory map and social story for the website
4. Assemble a sensory kit for the venue
5. Trial a fortnightly low-sensory session
6. Get listed on the Accessible Australia App, Accessibull Eats, and Be Like Buddy
7. Submit to What's On Melbourne's accessible venue guides
8. Consider KultureCity or Aspect accreditation as a longer-term credibility signal

## 9. Measurement (5 min)

Baseline now, re-measure in 90 days:

- Google Business Profile views and direction clicks
- Access page traffic and time on page
- Bookings mentioning access needs in the notes field
- Review sentiment (search reviews for access-related keywords)
- Social media reach on access-themed posts versus baseline
- Number of sensory kit requests
- Attendance at low-sensory sessions
- Enquiries or bookings from disability organisations
- Listings and reviews on accessibility directories

## 10. 30 / 60 / 90 Day Action Plan (5 min)

### 30 Days
- Access page live on the website with photos
- Google Business Profile accessibility attributes updated, access photos added
- Staff briefed with a one-page FAQ sheet on access features
- 2 social media posts about the accessible experience
- Autism SA Autism Friendly Charter registration submitted
- Sensory kit assembled and available at the bar
- Travellers Aid contacted to let them know Arbory is accessible

### 60 Days
- Video walkthrough filmed, captioned, and published
- First low-sensory session trialled
- Community partner soft-launch: invite Amaze, Expression Australia, Inclusion Melbourne, VALID for a complimentary experience
- Local media pitch sent (Broadsheet, Time Out, The Age Good Food)
- Accessible Australia App and Accessibull Eats listings submitted
- Staff awareness training delivered (Amaze, I CAN Network, or Aspect)

### 90 Days
- Review metrics against baseline
- Gather feedback from community partners and adjust
- Submit to What's On Melbourne accessible guides and Visit Victoria
- Social story published on the website
- Plan first Auslan-interpreted live event
- Assess interest in Aspect or KultureCity accreditation
