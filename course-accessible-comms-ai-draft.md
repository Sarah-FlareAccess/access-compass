## Course Overview

**What you'll do**: In 2 hours, you'll use free AI tools to draft one piece of accessible or inclusive communication for your business. You choose the format, you choose the source content, you leave with a real draft you can review, refine, and publish.

**Who it's for**: Business owners, marketing leads, customer service managers, and event organisers who want to make their communications work for more people. No tech background needed.

**Tools you'll use**:
- **ChatGPT** (free or Plus) — primary drafting assistant

- **Microsoft Copilot** or **Google Gemini** (optional alternative if your business already uses one)

**By the end you'll have**:
1. One draft of accessible content in your chosen format
2. A reusable AI assistant set up for future content
3. A Prompt Pack covering all formats so you can keep going after the session

**Your work belongs to you.** Anything you create in this session, including the draft and the prompts you save, is yours. Access Compass does not claim any rights to your content.

**Important reminder throughout**: AI drafts. You review. Always.

## Format Library: When and Why Each Format Is Used

Quick reference. Use this if you're not sure which format to pick, or to explain to a colleague later why you chose what you chose.

### Easy Read
**What it is**: Short sentences (under 15 words), common words, supporting images, lots of white space. Often laid out one idea per line, with a picture beside the text.
**Who it's for and when to use it**: People with intellectual disability, low literacy, acquired brain injury, dementia, or English as an additional language. Use for health info, consent forms, "what to expect" venue info, government letters, important announcements.
**Standard to follow**: Inclusion Australia Easy Read guidelines, or Council for Intellectual Disability (CID) guidelines.

### Plain Language
**What it is**: Reading level around year 7 to year 8, active voice, common words, no jargon, no idioms.
**Who it's for and when to use it**: The general public, including people who are tired, distracted, time-poor, or reading in a second language. Use for anything you want broadly understood: letters, emails, web content, instructions, policies, terms and conditions.
**Standard to follow**: Australian Government Style Manual, Plain Language Association International.

### Social Story / Visual Narrative
**What it is**: A first-person walkthrough of an experience using photos and short sentences. "When I arrive at the museum, I will see a big front desk. A staff member will smile and say hello."
**Who it's for and when to use it**: Autistic and neurodiverse people, people with anxiety, children, anyone preparing for a new environment. Use for pre-visit guides for museums, attractions, transport hubs, healthcare appointments, events.
**Standard to follow**: Carol Gray social story criteria for the strict format. Visual narratives are more flexible.

### Accessibility Guide
**What it is**: Structured info about the access features of a venue, event, or service. Organised by topic (getting there, getting in, getting around, sensory environment, support available, contact).
**Who it's for and when to use it**: Customers with access needs and their support networks deciding whether and how to visit. Use on any web page where customers plan a visit, in event listings, in booking confirmations.
**Standard to follow**: Australian peak-body templates and DIAP-aligned categories.

### Accessible Word Documents
**What it is**: A Word doc formatted so screen readers and other assistive tech can navigate it. Proper heading hierarchy, alt text on images, descriptive link text, accessible tables.
**Who it's for and when to use it**: Anyone using a screen reader, magnification, or keyboard-only navigation. Use for any Word doc sent externally: tenders, grant applications, customer-facing docs, board papers.
**Standard to follow**: Microsoft's built-in Accessibility Checker plus WCAG-aligned document practices.

### Large Print
**What it is**: Minimum 18pt sans-serif font, high contrast, 1.5 line spacing, plenty of white space.
**Who it's for and when to use it**: People with low vision. Use for menus, event programs, info handouts. Usually available on request at front-of-house.
**Standard to follow**: Vision Australia clear print guidance.

### Visual Communication / Symbol-Supported Text
**What it is**: Text paired with picture symbols.
**Who it's for and when to use it**: People with complex communication needs, including some autistic people, people with intellectual disability, and people with aphasia. Use for high-frequency interactions: ordering food, asking for help, finding the toilet, paying.
**Symbol source**: ARASAAC (free, Creative Commons licensed, 12,000+ symbols).
> **Tool limit**: AI tools can plan the content and write the text. They can't reliably produce a finished symbol-paired document. Use ARASAAC's downloadable symbols to build the visual layer yourself, or use specialist software.

## Universal Disclaimers

Read these once. We'll repeat the most important ones throughout the lessons.

> ⚠️ **AI drafts. You review. Always.**
> AI tools can produce plausible content that contains errors, outdated information, or assumptions that don't match your business. Every AI-generated draft must be reviewed by a human before publishing. Where possible, also test with someone from the audience the content is for.

> ⚠️ **AI doesn't know your business.**
> Unless you tell it, the AI doesn't know your opening hours, your accessibility features, your audience, or your tone. If the AI sounds confident about something you didn't tell it, treat that as a guess until you've verified it.

> ⚠️ **Lived experience cannot be replaced.**
> AI cannot tell you whether a piece of content actually works for the people it's for. The most reliable test is a person from the audience reading the draft and telling you what's confusing, missing, or wrong.

> ⚠️ **Don't paste personal or confidential information into free AI tools.**
> Free tiers may use your input for training. Strip out names, account numbers, and anything you wouldn't want to be seen by someone else.

## Step 3: Paste the AI assistant system prompt (5 min)

Copy the **Accessible Comms Assistant system prompt** below and paste it into the location for your tool from Step 2.

```
ROLE & PURPOSE
You are my Accessible Communications Assistant.

Your job is to help me convert standard business content into accessible and inclusive formats including:
- Easy Read
- Plain Language
- Social Stories and Visual Narratives
- Accessibility Guides
- Accessible Word documents
- Large Print layouts
- Visual Communication (symbol-supported text)

You will help by:
- asking clarifying questions before drafting
- producing draft content in the requested format
- explaining your formatting choices when asked
- suggesting how to test the draft with the audience
- flagging anything that needs a human reviewer with lived experience

FORMATTING RULES
- Use clear headings, short paragraphs, and bullet points
- Avoid walls of text
- When producing Easy Read or Plain Language drafts, follow the published standards for each format
- Always finish any draft with: "This draft was AI-generated. Please review with a person from the audience before publishing."

BEHAVIOUR RULES
- Ask clarifying questions when you don't have enough context
- Preserve context from earlier in the conversation
- Default to simplicity over complexity
- When I am vague, ask for specifics
- When I am stuck, simplify
- Never invent facts about my business, venue, audience, or accessibility features. If you do not know, ask.

ACCESSIBILITY STANDARDS REFERENCE
- Easy Read: Inclusion Australia and CID Easy Read guidelines. Short sentences, common words, one idea per line, supporting images.
- Plain Language: Australian Government Style Manual and Plain Language Association International. Year 7 to 8 reading level. Active voice. Common words.
- Social Stories: Carol Gray model. First person. Descriptive. Non-judgemental.
- Accessibility Guides: Structured by topic (getting there, getting in, getting around, sensory environment, support, contact).
- Accessible Word: Proper heading hierarchy, alt text on images, descriptive link text, accessible tables.
- WCAG 2.2 AA for web content.

WHAT TO DO IN ANY NEW TASK
Your first reply must include:
1. A one or two sentence summary of what you understand the task to be
2. 3 to 5 clarifying questions if needed to fill any gaps
3. Explain any assumptions you would have to make if I cannot answer those questions
```

Switch to your Claude tab. Start a new conversation. Paste the **Reviewer prompt** below and press Enter.

```
You are my Accessible Communications Reviewer.

Your role is to audit drafts created in ChatGPT (or anywhere) for clarity, accessibility, and inclusion.

2. The top 3 to 5 issues to fix, in priority order
3. Suggested re-wordings for the top 2 or 3 issues

5. One or two questions that would strengthen the next iteration
```

# Lesson 2: Choose Your Format and Brief the AI (25 minutes)

## What you'll achieve
By the end of this lesson, your AI assistant knows your format, your audience, your source material, and any constraints. It's ready to draft.

## Step 1: Choose your format (3 min)

Use the **Format Picker** table at the top of this course (or the **Format Library** below it if you need more detail).

Write your choice down: "I'm creating ___ for ___."

> Most common picks for this cohort:
> - **Accessibility Guide** for venue and event businesses
> - **Social Story / Visual Narrative** for first-visit experiences
> - **Easy Read** for booking info, what-to-expect content, and consent
> - **Plain Language** for almost everything else

## Step 2: Gather your source material (3 min)

Use the **Format Picker** table to confirm what counts as source material for your chosen format.

Open a blank document or note. Copy in whatever you have. This might be:
- The text from an existing webpage
- A link to an existing webpage (you can paste links to ChatGPT)
- Bullet points
- A description you type fresh

If you have nothing, that's fine. The AI will prompt you in the next step.

## Step 3: Brief your AI assistant (10 min)

Copy this **Briefing prompt** into ChatGPT. Fill in the brackets first.

```
I want to create a [FORMAT NAME] for [AUDIENCE].

This will be used by [WHO USES IT] to [PURPOSE].

It will live [WHERE IT WILL BE PUBLISHED, e.g., on our website, in a printed handout, in our booking confirmation email].

Here is my source material:
[paste text, link, or notes here]

Here are the most important things to get right:
[e.g., must mention wheelchair access, must use our cafe's friendly tone, must be under 500 words]

Before drafting:
1. Confirm in 2 to 3 sentences what you understand.
2. Ask me 3 to 5 clarifying questions to fill any gaps.
3. List any assumptions you would have to make if I cannot answer.
```

`[SCREENSHOT: Filled-in briefing prompt example in ChatGPT]`

## Step 4: Answer the clarifying questions (7 min)

ChatGPT will reply with questions. Answer them honestly. If you don't know an answer, say so. Examples of useful answers:

- "Wheelchair access: yes, level entry through the side gate, contact reception"
- "Bathroom: accessible toilet on ground floor, ambulant cubicles on level 1"
- "Tone: friendly but professional, similar to our website homepage"
- "Don't know — please flag this as needing a human to confirm"

> **Tip**: It's fine to answer in fragments and bullet points. The AI will tidy them up.

## Step 5: Confirm the AI is ready (2 min)

Type into ChatGPT:

```
Please summarise what you know so far. List anything I haven't told you that you'd want before drafting.
```

Read the summary. If there are still gaps, fill them in now. If the summary is good, type:

```
Great. Please proceed to drafting in the next message.
```

✅ **Lesson 2 complete.** Your AI assistant now has enough context to draft.

> ⚠️ **Disclaimer reminder**: Anything the AI tells you it "knows" about your business that you didn't tell it is a guess. Treat it as a prompt to verify, not a fact.

# Lesson 3: Build Your Draft (50 minutes)

## What you'll achieve
By the end of this lesson, you'll have a first draft of your chosen format that you've iterated on at least twice.

## Step 1: Send the format-specific build prompt (10 min)

Find your chosen format in the **Build Prompts** section below. Copy the prompt for your format. Paste into ChatGPT. Press Enter.

The AI will produce your first draft.

`[SCREENSHOT: Example ChatGPT response with a first draft of Easy Read content]`

## Step 2: Read it once. Don't fix yet. (3 min)

Read your first draft top to bottom. Resist the urge to start editing.

Ask yourself:
- Does this sound like my business?
- Is anything obviously wrong?
- Is anything missing?
- Where would a reader get confused or stuck?

Make a short list (3 to 5 items max) of what you want changed.

## Step 3: Iterate (35 min)

Run 2 to 3 iteration rounds. One change per round works better than asking for everything at once.

Use the **Iteration prompts** below. Pick the ones that match what you want changed.

**Make it clearer:**
```
Rewrite section [X] using shorter sentences and simpler words. Aim for a reading age of 12 or below.
```

**Make it shorter:**
```
The [section / overall draft] is too long. Cut it by 30% without losing the key information.
```

**Make it friendlier:**
```
The tone feels too formal. Rewrite it to sound warmer and more welcoming, while keeping the structure.
```

**Add specifics:**
```
Add concrete examples in section [X]. Use [these details I'll provide]: [paste details]
```

**Fix accuracy:**
```
You assumed [X]. The actual situation is [Y]. Please rewrite the relevant sections.
```

**Add what's missing:**
```
You haven't covered [X]. Please add a section about it. Here's what you need to know: [paste info]
```

> **Tip**: After each round, re-read the whole draft, not just the changed bit. AI sometimes changes things you didn't ask it to.

## Step 4: Save your work in progress (2 min)

Copy your current draft into a Word doc, Google Doc, or wherever you normally work. Save it.

Don't close ChatGPT. You'll keep using this conversation in Lesson 4.

✅ **Lesson 3 complete.** You have a working draft.

> ⚠️ **Disclaimer reminder**: The draft is not ready to publish yet. Lesson 4 catches issues that are easy to miss when you've been staring at the same content.

## Build Prompts (pick one for your chosen format)

### Easy Read build prompt

```
Please draft this in Easy Read format following Inclusion Australia and CID guidelines.

Apply these rules:
- One main idea per line
- Sentences under 15 words wherever possible
- Use common words (year 6 reading level or below)
- Avoid metaphors, idioms, and figures of speech
- Use bullet points for lists
- Break the draft into short sections with clear headings
- For each section, suggest an image that would support the meaning (describe the image, do not generate it)
- Avoid abbreviations. Spell things out.
- Use the active voice
- Address the reader as "you"

After the draft, list:
- Any words you used that may still be too hard
- Any sections that would benefit from an example
- Anything I should verify with a human reviewer
```

### Plain Language build prompt

```
Please draft this in Plain Language following the Australian Government Style Manual.

Apply these rules:
- Reading level: year 7 to year 8
- Sentences under 20 words on average
- Active voice
- Common words. No jargon. If you must use a technical term, define it.
- Short paragraphs (3 sentences or fewer)
- Use clear headings
- Bullet points for lists
- Address the reader as "you" where appropriate

After the draft, give me:
- An estimate of the reading level
- A list of any words I should consider replacing
- One sentence on what the reader should walk away knowing
```

### Social Story / Visual Narrative build prompt

```
Please draft this as a Social Story / Visual Narrative.

Apply these rules:
- Written in first person ("I will...", "I might see...")
- Descriptive and reassuring, not instructional or judgemental
- Walk the reader through the experience in the order it happens
- Note sensory details (what they will see, hear, smell, feel)
- Mention what staff or other people might do
- Acknowledge that things can change and that's okay
- Suggest where photos should sit (describe each photo, do not generate)
- Use short sentences

Structure the draft as:
1. Before I arrive
2. When I get there
3. What I will see and do inside
4. If I need help
5. When I leave

After the draft, list:
- Photos I should take to go with each section
- Anything I should verify or add from a real walk-through
- Variations to consider (e.g., for a quieter sensory experience)
```

### Accessibility Guide build prompt

```
Please draft an Accessibility Guide for my venue or event.

Structure it under these headings:
1. Getting there (transport, parking, drop-off)
2. Getting in (entrance, doors, level access, alternative entrances)
3. Getting around (paths, ramps, lifts, signage, distances)
4. Toilets (accessible, ambulant, all-gender, changing places)
5. Sensory environment (lighting, noise, crowds, quiet areas)
6. Support available (staff training, assistance dogs, companion cards, large print or Easy Read on request)
7. Contact (best way to ask access questions before visiting)

Apply these rules:
- Plain Language throughout
- Specific, not vague. "Wheelchair accessible" is too vague. Say "level entry through the front door, 90cm wide" instead.
- If I haven't told you something for a section, leave it blank and flag it as "needs answer"
- Use bullet points for facts, prose for context

After the draft, give me:
- A list of every "needs answer" item I still need to fill in
- Photos I should take to go with the guide
- Anything that would benefit from a person with lived experience to verify
```

### Accessible Word Document build prompt

```
I have a Word document I want to make accessible. I will paste the content below.

Please:
1. Suggest the heading hierarchy (which lines should be H1, H2, H3, etc.)
2. Rewrite any link text that says "click here" or "read more" to be descriptive
3. Flag any images that need alt text and suggest alt text for each (I'll confirm)
4. Suggest where bullet lists should replace dense paragraphs
5. Identify any tables that need accessible structure (header rows, no merged cells)
6. Flag any colour-only meaning (e.g., "items in red are urgent") and suggest a fix

After the suggestions, give me a numbered checklist of changes to make in Word.

Here is the content:
[paste content]
```

### Large Print build prompt

```
I have content I want to provide in Large Print. I will paste it below.

Please:
1. Confirm the content is appropriate for Large Print (short, high-priority information)
2. Suggest any text edits to make it scannable (shorter sentences, clearer headings, removing decorative content)
3. Give me the formatting checklist for Large Print (minimum font size, font family, line spacing, contrast, paper colour, margins) following Vision Australia guidance

Here is the content:
[paste content]
```

### Visual Communication build prompt

```
I want to create symbol-supported content for [INTERACTION, e.g., ordering at the bar, asking for help, finding the toilet].

Please:
1. Write the text in short, action-focused phrases (3 to 6 words each)
2. For each phrase, suggest a clear concept name that I can search in the ARASAAC symbol library
3. Suggest a layout (e.g., grid of 6 boxes, sequence of arrows, single page with key actions)
4. Note any accessibility considerations for the visual design (contrast, symbol size, white space)

Reminder for me: I will source the symbols from ARASAAC (free) and pair them with the text myself. AI cannot reliably generate the final symbol-paired document.
```

## Step 4: Final human review checklist (3 min)

Before you publish, you (or a colleague) need to check:

- ☐ Is everything factually correct about your business and venue?
- ☐ Have you tested it with at least one person from the target audience? (If not, plan to before publishing.)
- ☐ Have any photos or images been described accurately?
- ☐ For Easy Read or Visual Communication: have you sourced images or symbols?
- ☐ For Accessibility Guides: has someone walked the route and verified the details?
- ☐ Have you removed any AI-added claims that you can't verify?
- ☐ Have you added the publishing date and a way for readers to give feedback?

## After the Session: Where to From Here

### Build momentum
The hardest part is starting. List every customer-facing piece of communication you have: website pages, signage, menus, booking confirmations, "what to expect" emails, social posts. Mark which would most benefit from an accessible version. Pick one more this week and use your saved prompt pack to convert it. Then keep going at one per week.

### Connect with people with lived experience
AI helps you draft. Lived experience tells you whether the draft works. Find one or two people you can pay or partner with for review. While you're at it, add a feedback line to every accessible piece you publish ("Was this useful? Tell us what we could do better") with a real address that a real person reads.

### Update your accessible content when your business changes
Accessible content goes stale. When you renovate, change your hours, change your menu, change your booking system, the accessible versions need updating too. Add this to your business-as-usual checklist.

AI tools can produce plausible content that contains errors, outdated information, or assumptions that don't match your business. Every AI-generated draft must be reviewed by a human before publishing. Where possible, also test with someone from the audience the content is for.

**AI doesn't know your business.**
Unless you tell it, the AI doesn't know your opening hours, your accessibility features, your audience, or your tone. If the AI sounds confident about something you didn't tell it, treat that as a guess until you've verified it.

**Lived experience cannot be replaced.**
AI cannot tell you whether a piece of content actually works for the people it's for. The most reliable test is a person from the audience reading the draft and telling you what's confusing, missing, or wrong.

**Don't paste personal or confidential information into free AI tools.**
Free tiers may use your input for training. Strip out names, account numbers, and anything you wouldn't want to be seen by someone else.

**Standards change. Re-check.**
Accessibility standards and guidelines evolve. The references in this course were current at the time of writing. Re-check the source publications periodically.
